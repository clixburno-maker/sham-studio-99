
One Aircraft Carrier Against the Entire Japanese Fleet

It's 06:58 AM  on October 25, 1944.
Commander Ernest Edwin Evans, thirty-six years old, stands on the bridge of USS Johnston — a Fletcher-class destroyer riding the gray swells eight miles east of Samar Island, Philippines — and watches the horizon turn into ships.
The colored splashes arrive before the sound does. Red. Yellow. Green. Columns of water the height of buildings erupt across the formation's bow, each color belonging to a different Japanese gun crew so their spotters can read the fall of shot. Evans watches the red ones walk toward him.

He does not have orders to attack.
What he has is this: six escort carriers running at 18 knots directly ahead of him, their flat-topped hulls riding low, carrying planes armed for shore support instead of anti-ship combat. They have bombs in their bays meant for jungle roads and troop concentrations, not for battleship armor. Rear Admiral Clifton Sprague has ordered the group east, into the wind, so they can launch whatever they have. But the Japanese — four battleships, six heavy cruisers, two light cruisers, eleven destroyers — are already at 34,000 yards and closing at 30 knots. The math is not arithmetic. It is a verdict.
Evans knows one other thing. The carriers at his back are what stand between Vice Admiral Kurita's fleet and the soldiers on the beaches at Leyte. MacArthur's men. The beachhead. The entire Philippine campaign. Halsey has taken the fleet north. The heavy ships of the Seventh Fleet are south in Surigao Strait. There is nothing between Kurita's battleships and 130,000 American soldiers except six slow, thin-skinned escort carriers, three destroyers, and four destroyer escorts.
Evans turns to his helmsman.
He does not ask permission.
He orders a turn to port.

Johnston breaks northeast — away from the carriers, toward the Japanese fleet — at flank speed.
Ensign Robert Hagen, the gunnery officer, watches the lead Japanese cruisers crawl toward them across the water and says, later, that he felt like little David without a slingshot.
At 16,000 yards, Evans orders the 5-inch battery to open fire.
The Japanese shells are still falling short, but they're walking in, and Evans begins to zigzag — not to evade, but to make Johnston's smoke screen a moving wall. She cuts across the front of the carrier formation at twenty-five knots, laying a black curtain 2,500 yards wide between Sprague's carriers and the Japanese guns. The colored salvos begin to fall behind the smoke. For a moment — one fragile moment — the carriers disappear.
Then Evans orders all ten torpedoes readied.

The range closes to 10,000 yards.
The Japanese cruiser Kumano fills the range finder. Evans watches her bow wave, calculates, and gives the firing solution.
"Fire torpedoes."
Johnston shudders down her length. Ten Mark 15s punch into the water.
Hagen is still working his guns. In five minutes, Johnston fires more than 200 rounds of 5-inch at Kumano at near-maximum rate. The shells are too small to penetrate cruiser armor, but the sheer volume of fire puts Kumano's bridge crew on the deck and walks splashes across her superstructure. One torpedo finds her bow. The explosion tears it completely off.
The gunnery officer later says it looked like Kumano simply ran into a wall.
And then Yamato speaks.

Three 18.1-inch shells from Yamato's main battery — each one weighing 3,200 pounds, each capable of sinking a destroyer by proximity alone — hit Johnston aft below the waterline. The deck heaves. Turrets three, four, and five go dark instantly, their power cut. Three seconds later, three 6.1-inch shells from Yamato's secondary battery tear through the bridge. One catches Evans's left hand. Two fingers are gone. He registers this the way a man registers a door slamming somewhere in another room. He wraps the hand. He does not leave the bridge.
Johnston has gone from two engines to one. Her speed drops to 17 knots. She has no torpedoes. Three of her five guns are silent. Her steering has to be worked manually from the fantail by sailors turning a wheel by hand, connected to the rudder by nothing more technical than their arms and their backs.
Evans stays on the bridge.
"Commence firing on that cruiser," he orders, watching the heavy cruiser Chikuma hammer the Gambier Bay — the carrier on the outside of the formation, already falling behind, already bleeding smoke. "Draw her fire on us and away from Gambier Bay."
Hagen calls it the most courageous order he has ever heard.

For a single minute, Chikuma shifts her guns toward Johnston.
The carrier gets a breath.
Then Chikuma returns to Gambier Bay, and the shells begin walking up the carrier's waterline again, and Evans understands that his ship — one broken destroyer with one engine and two working guns — is not enough to save her.
Gambier Bay is listing. Gambier Bay is slowing. Gambier Bay is going to die.

A Japanese destroyer squadron appears out of the smoke.
Not one destroyer. Not two.
Seven.
Evans counts them through binoculars with one hand pressing cloth against the wound on the other. They are led by the light cruiser Yahagi, and they are moving to deliver a torpedo attack that will finish the carriers. Johnston, her bridge on fire, her fantail crew steering her by hand through an open hatch while Evans shouts down to them, turns directly toward the squadron.
She has no torpedoes.
She fires anyway.
Johnston opens up at Yahagi and her seven destroyers with her two remaining 5-inch guns, claiming twelve hits on the cruiser. The Japanese squadron turns broadside, brings every gun to bear, and answers in kind. A 6-inch shell shears through turret two. Everyone inside it dies.
The bridge becomes untenable — fires, the 40mm ready ammunition cooking off in chains. Evans moves his command to the fantail.
He shouts steering orders down through a hatch.
The men below turn the rudder by hand.

By 0930, Johnston is in the center of a circle.
Japanese battleships and cruisers and destroyers have surrounded her on three sides, firing from every bearing. A survivor will later say they moved around her like Indians attacking a prairie schooner, unaware, in his exhaustion, of the irony in his choice of words — because the man on Johnston's fantail shouting orders down through a hatch is half Cherokee and one-quarter Creek, born in Pawnee, Oklahoma, the grandson of people who knew what it was to make a last stand.
The last engine goes.
Then the last fire room.
Johnston slows.
She stops.
She sits dead in the water while the Japanese guns, no longer needing to lead their target, begin to land at will.
At 0945, Evans gives the order no captain wants to give.
"Abandon ship."

Men go into the water.
Johnston takes the last shots.
At 1010, she rolls to starboard and begins to slide by the bow. The guns go under still pointed northeast. A wreck-diver who finds her seventy-five years later will say the guns still look like they're fighting.
And then the Yukikaze comes.
She is a Japanese destroyer that has been in this battle since the beginning. She comes alongside Johnston at 1,000 yards, fires one last shell to make certain she sinks — and then her commander, Terauchi Masamichi, orders his crew to muster on the starboard rail. They line up smartly. They stand at attention. Masamichi salutes the American ship. His sailors salute.
In a lifeboat nearby, survivors watch.
One of them sees Evans — already wounded, already dying, already adrift — raise his hand from the lifeboat and salute back.
With tears in his eyes.

Of Johnston's 327 men, 186 were lost. Roughly 50 died to enemy fire. Another 45 died on rafts from their wounds. Ninety-two, including Commander Ernest Edwin Evans, were alive in the water when the ship went down — and were never heard from again.
Evans was never found.
His body was last seen in a lifeboat. The sea took him somewhere that has no name on any chart, two miles above the wreck of his ship, which rests 21,000 feet beneath the surface of the Philippine Sea — the deepest shipwreck ever discovered, confirmed by hull number 557 in 2021, its guns still pointing northeast.
The myth has it that it was Kongō's 14-inch guns that first hit Johnston at 0730. Japanese records show Kongō was blind in a rain squall and could not even see Hoel at 9,000 yards. It was Yamato who hit Johnston. The largest battleship ever built is the ship that took Evans's fingers. The historical record corrects this only in footnotes. It hasn't changed the story. The story already knows what it is.

At the same moment Johnston sank, Vice Admiral Takeo Kurita — looking at his radar, at the smoke, at the relentless torpedo wakes and the planes that kept coming back with empty bomb bays pretending to attack — decided he was fighting a fleet carrier task force. He ordered the retreat. He was wrong about everything except the part that mattered: that whoever was out there was not going to stop.
The man Kurita could not identify was standing on a fantail with two fingers missing, shouting down through a hatch.
